How Much Does Indoor Golf Cost Per Hour?

If you're wondering what you'll actually pay to swing a club at an indoor golf simulator, you've come to the right place. The cost of indoor golf has become increasingly competitive, and understanding the pricing structure will help you budget for your next session—whether you're looking to improve your swing during winter or escape the weather year-round.

Typical Hourly Rates for Indoor Golf Simulators

Most indoor golf facilities in the United States charge between $30 and $100 per hour for simulator bay rental. The variation depends on several factors we'll break down below, but this range gives you a realistic baseline for planning your visit.

Budget-friendly venues typically fall in the $30–$50 per hour range, while premium facilities with high-end technology or prime locations may charge $75–$100+ per hour. Some upscale golf clubs and resort-style simulators can even exceed this, but most recreational golfers will find solid options within the standard pricing window.

What Affects Indoor Golf Pricing?

Several variables impact what you'll pay when you book a simulator bay:

  • Simulator Technology. Facilities using premium launch monitors like TrackMan and Foresight GCQuad typically charge more than those with entry-level systems like SkyTrak or Bushnell Launch Pro. Full Swing and Toptracer venues fall somewhere in the middle. The data accuracy and graphics quality justify the higher rates.
  • Location and Market. Urban centers and major metropolitan areas command higher prices than suburban or rural facilities. A bay in downtown Chicago or New York will likely cost more than one in a smaller market.
  • Time of Day. Peak hours (evenings and weekends) are often pricier than off-peak times. You might save 20–30% by booking a weekday morning session.
  • Membership and Packages. Many facilities offer monthly memberships or punch cards that reduce your per-hour cost significantly—sometimes down to $20–$40 per hour if you commit to regular play.
  • Group Rates. Bringing friends typically qualifies you for discounts. Outings and corporate events may get special pricing.
  • Food and Beverage. Some venues bundle drinks or snacks into their bay rental, while others charge separately. This can add $10–$25 to your session.

Making the Most of Your Investment

Understanding pricing is one thing—getting real value is another. Here's how to stretch your indoor golf budget:

Book During Off-Peak Hours. If your schedule allows, morning and midweek sessions save you money. Many facilities offer "happy hour" pricing in the afternoon.

Join a Membership Program. If you plan to visit more than twice a month, a membership almost always pays for itself. You're looking at monthly dues between $100–$300, depending on the facility, but per-session costs drop dramatically.

Split the Cost. Indoor golf is social. Bring three friends to share a bay, and you're cutting your hourly cost by 75%. Four golfers taking turns on one simulator makes the math work beautifully for your wallet.

Ask About Packages. Some venues offer discounted rates if you book multiple hours at once, or they bundle simulator time with golf lessons from a PGA professional.

Is Indoor Golf Worth the Cost?

Consider what you're paying for: instant swing data from systems like TrackMan and GCQuad, access to hundreds of virtual courses, and year-round practice without weather constraints. You're getting legitimate golf improvement tools that used to be exclusive to tour pros and high-end clubs.

For serious golfers, the $40–$70 per hour range represents exceptional value when you factor in equipment quality, convenience, and the ability to track your progress over time.
